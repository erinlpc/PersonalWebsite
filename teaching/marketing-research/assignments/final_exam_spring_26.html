The Scenario

You have just joined the analytics team at Evergreen Subscriptions, a mid-sized direct-to-consumer company that sells curated monthly product boxes. Your first assignment comes directly from the Chief Marketing Officer, who wants to understand what drives monthly customer spending and whether the company’s current email and promotional strategies are actually working.

The company has been operating on a set of standing assumptions about their customer base:

Variable Company Assumption
Average monthly spend $80.00
Typical emails sent per month 4
Promotion effectiveness Flat $10 lift in spending
Engagement effect Linear and uniform across all customers

Your job is to put these assumptions to the test using a sample of 1,500 customer records. Along the way, you will build increasingly sophisticated models — adding engagement, income, promotion interactions, nonlinear effects, and tenure — to understand not just whether these drivers matter, but how they matter and for whom.

Your CMO is analytically curious but not a statistician. Throughout the exam, she will ask questions like:

  • “Does sending more emails actually move the needle, or are we just assuming it does?”
  • “Does our promo work the same way for every customer?”
  • “Is there a point of diminishing returns on engagement or is more always better?”
  • “Should we be treating all customers the same, or are there meaningful segments we’re missing?”

Your answers need to be based on the data and your analyses.

Section B: Tenure as a grouped categorical predictor

The firm’s executive has asked that customer tenure be presented in terms of three groups: Short (fewer than 12 months), Medium (12 to 24 months inclusive), and Long (more than 24 months). Use case_when() from the dplyr package to create Tenure_Group, then confirm it is coded as a factor before fitting models.

exam2 <- exam2 %>%
  mutate(
    Tenure_Group = case_when(
      Tenure_Months < 12  ~ "Short",
      Tenure_Months <= 24 ~ "Medium",
      Tenure_Months > 24  ~ "Long"
    )
  )

exam2$Tenure_Group <- as.factor(exam2$Tenure_Group)
class(exam2$Tenure_Group)
levels(exam2$Tenure_Group)

Model_7_compact: Monthly_Spend ~ 1

Model_7: Monthly_Spend ~ Tenure_Group

Model_7_continuous: Monthly_Spend ~ tenure_c

Model_7_compact    <- lm(Monthly_Spend ~ 1,            data = exam2)
Model_7            <- lm(Monthly_Spend ~ Tenure_Group, data = exam2)
Model_7_continuous <- lm(Monthly_Spend ~ tenure_c,     data = exam2)

summary(Model_7)
anova(Model_7_compact, Model_7)
summary(Model_7_continuous)

Q54 (text entry) — From Model_7, report the PRE for Tenure_Group as a predictor of Monthly_Spend. Round to four decimal places.

Q55 (text entry) — From Model_7_continuous, report the PRE for tenure_c as a predictor of Monthly_Spend. Round to four decimal places.

Q56 (MC) — Comparing the PRE from Model_7 (binned) to Model_7_continuous (continuous), what happened when tenure was converted to three groups?
